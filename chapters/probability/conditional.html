4  Conditional Probability

4.1 The question

A rapid test for some disease is described as 95% accurate.

You take the test. It comes back positive.

How worried should you be?

If you said “95% chance I have it”, you’re wrong. The right answer might surprise you. To get there, we need conditional probability — the math of updating what you believe in light of new information.

We’ll build the answer in three steps:

  1. Write a tiny simulation that mirrors the real situation.
  2. Look at the numbers it produces and notice the pattern.
  3. Then introduce the formula — at which point it’ll feel like the simulation is just executing the formula, not the other way around.
Note▶ Show the math — Why this section matters

Conditional probability is the door to everything important in probability that comes after: Bayes’ rule, independence, Markov chains, likelihood, Bayesian inference, every probabilistic ML model. If you only deeply learn one section in this chapter, this is the one.

4.2 Step 1 — Simulate the test

Imagine a population of 100,000 people. We need three numbers to define the world:

What Value Meaning
Disease prevalence 1% Out of 100,000 people, 1,000 actually sick.
True-positive rate (sensitivity) 95% Sick people test positive 95% of the time.
True-negative rate (specificity) 95% Healthy people test negative 95% of the time.

That second + third number is what “95% accurate” usually means in advertising. The first number — prevalence — is the one nobody mentions, and it’s the one that matters most.

Let’s run it:

import numpy as np

rng = np.random.default_rng(seed=42)

population = 100_000
prevalence = 0.01            # 1% of people are actually sick
sensitivity = 0.95           # P(test+ | sick)
specificity = 0.95           # P(test- | healthy)

# Simulate who is actually sick
is_sick = rng.random(population) < prevalence

# Simulate test results — different probabilities depending on truth
test_positive = np.where(
    is_sick,
    rng.random(population) < sensitivity,    # sick: 95% chance of +
    rng.random(population) < (1 - specificity)  # healthy: 5% chance of +
)

print(f"Total people simulated:        {population:,}")
print(f"Actually sick:                 {is_sick.sum():,}")
print(f"Tested positive:               {test_positive.sum():,}")
print(f"Tested positive AND sick:      {(test_positive & is_sick).sum():,}")
Total people simulated:        100,000
Actually sick:                 1,047
Tested positive:               5,846
Tested positive AND sick:      982

Look at those last two lines. Most people who tested positive are not actually sick. Let’s compute the answer to our original question:

n_positive = test_positive.sum()
n_positive_and_sick = (test_positive & is_sick).sum()

p_sick_given_positive = n_positive_and_sick / n_positive

print(f"P(sick | tested positive) = {p_sick_given_positive:.1%}")
P(sick | tested positive) = 16.8%

About 16% — not 95%. If you tested positive on this 95%-accurate test for a 1%-prevalence disease, your odds of actually being sick are roughly one in six.

That should feel deeply wrong on first read. Let’s see why it’s right.

4.3 Step 2 — Stare at the table

The simulation gave us four kinds of people. Let’s count each:

import pandas as pd

table = pd.crosstab(
    pd.Series(is_sick, name="actually sick").map({True: "sick", False: "healthy"}),
    pd.Series(test_positive, name="test result").map({True: "positive", False: "negative"}),
    margins=True, margins_name="total"
)
print(table)
test result    negative  positive   total
actually sick                            
healthy           94089      4864   98953
sick                 65       982    1047
total             94154      5846  100000

The positive column has two kinds of people in it:

  • True positives — actually sick, correctly flagged. (~950 people)
  • False positives — actually healthy, but the test misfired. (~5,000 people)

Even though the false-positive rate is small (5%), it’s applied to the huge healthy population (99,000). Five percent of a huge group dwarfs ninety-five percent of a tiny group. So the positive column is dominated by healthy people.

Conditional probability is just restricting attention to one column (or one row) of this table:

“What fraction of the positive column is actually sick?”

\;\;\;\;= \dfrac{\text{true positives}}{\text{all positives}}

\;\;\;\;\approx \dfrac{950}{950 + 5000} \approx 16\%.

That’s it. That’s the whole idea. Nothing to memorise yet — we just counted.

4.4 Step 3 — Now the formula

Let S = “actually sick” and T = “tested positive”. The phrase “given that the test was positive” is conditional probability, written P(S \mid T). It’s defined as:

P(S \mid T) \;=\; \frac{P(S \cap T)}{P(T)}

Look at the simulation again:

  • P(S \cap T) — fraction of the whole table that is both sick and positive: 950 / 100{,}000
  • P(T) — fraction of the whole table that is positive: 5950 / 100{,}000
  • Their ratio: 950 / 5950 \approx 16\% ✓

The 100,000 cancels. Restricting to a column is exactly what dividing by P(T) does. The formula is just bookkeeping for the table you’ve already seen.

Note▶ Show the math — Formal definition and where it comes from

Definition. For events A and B with P(B) > 0:

P(A \mid B) \;\triangleq\; \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}.

Why this is well-defined. The function Q(A) = P(A \mid B), viewed as a function of A on the same sample space \Omega, is itself a probability measure:

  1. Q(A) \ge 0 for every event A, because P(A \cap B) \ge 0 and P(B) > 0.
  2. Q(\Omega) = P(\Omega \cap B)/P(B) = P(B)/P(B) = 1.
  3. Countable additivity. For disjoint events A_1, A_2, \ldots:

Q\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i\right) \;=\; \frac{P\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i \cap B\right)}{P(B)} \;=\; \frac{\sum_i P(A_i \cap B)}{P(B)} \;=\; \sum_i Q(A_i),

using countable additivity of P on the disjoint sets A_i \cap B.

So all three Kolmogorov axioms hold for Q. Conditioning on B produces a new probability measure that lives on the same sample space but treats B as the new “universe” — events outside B get probability zero, and events inside B are rescaled so they sum to one.

Why P(B) > 0. If P(B) = 0, the ratio is undefined. Conditioning on probability-zero events is possible in measure theory (via regular conditional distributions) but requires more machinery. For everything in this book, P(B) > 0 is enforced.

4.5 Why the result was so counterintuitive

Most people, asked “what’s the probability you’re sick given a positive test?”, silently substitute the reverse question: “what’s the probability of testing positive given that you’re sick?” That second quantity is the sensitivity — 95%.

These two probabilities, P(S \mid T) and P(T \mid S), are very different in general. Confusing them is so common it has a name — the prosecutor’s fallacy — and it shows up in courtrooms, medical decision-making, and basically every bad statistics-in-the-news story you’ll ever read.

The trick that converts one to the other is Bayes’ rule, which is what the next section is about. But you’ve already seen its essence: it’s the formula that re-weights your belief by the prevalence (the prior).

4.6 Three things you should take away

  1. Conditional probability is restriction. “Given B” means “restrict the universe to B, then re-normalise.” The table makes this visible; the formula encodes it.

  2. The denominator matters more than the numerator. Small false-positive rates against large healthy populations can swamp small true-positive counts against tiny sick populations. Always ask: “what’s the base rate?”

  3. You can simulate first and derive second. This is going to be the pattern for the rest of the chapter. When a probability question feels slippery, write down a population, simulate it, count what’s in each bucket. The formulas are descriptions of those counts.

Tip▶ Show the math — Bayes’ rule, briefly

Once you have P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B)/P(B), swap A and B:

P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}.

Solving the first equation for P(A \cap B) and substituting:

P(A \mid B) \;=\; \frac{P(B \mid A)\, P(A)}{P(B)}.

That’s Bayes’ rule. In our example: A = S (sick), B = T (positive test):

P(S \mid T) \;=\; \frac{P(T \mid S)\, P(S)}{P(T)} \;=\; \frac{0.95 \times 0.01}{0.0595} \;\approx\; 0.16.

The 0.0595 in the denominator comes from the law of total probability: P(T) = P(T \mid S)\,P(S) + P(T \mid S^c)\,P(S^c) = 0.95 \cdot 0.01 + 0.05 \cdot 0.99.

4.7 Exercises

  1. Vary the prevalence. Re-run the simulation with prevalence = 50\% (a coin-flip baseline). What’s P(\text{sick} \mid +) now? Why does the answer change so dramatically?

  2. Improve the test. Keep prevalence at 1%, but raise specificity to 99.9%. What does P(\text{sick} \mid +) become? Which knob moves the answer faster — sensitivity or specificity? Why?

  3. The reverse question. From the simulation output, compute P(\text{tested positive} \mid \text{sick}) directly. Confirm it’s close to 0.95. Notice that this is not the same as the answer in step 3.
